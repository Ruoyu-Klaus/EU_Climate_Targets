Europe has a history of progressive environmental legislation, with the earliest measures dating back to 1996, when the EU adopted a target to limit the global temperature rise to below 2 °C, compared to pre-industrial levels.

Currently, the EU has set itself milestones for 2020, 2030 and 2050.

By the end of 2020, the EU aims to cut 20% in greenhouse gas emissions from 1990 levels.

In 2018, emissions were down by 23.2% across the EU, putting the EU firmly on track to meet its target at the end of 2020.

By 2030, the EU expects to cut greenhouse gas emissions by at least 40% from 1990 levels.

In November 2019 a new European Commission, the executive branch of the EU, took office. Ursula von der Leyen, the Commission’s first-ever female president, vowed for a green, geopolitical and digital EU in her speech before Parliament.

The European ‘Green Deal’ aims for at least a 50-55% emissions reduction by 2030.

The 'Green Deal' also committed Europe to becoming climate neutral by 2050. The proposals would be enshrine in law this summer. Climate neutrality means emissions will yield no net impact on the climate: climate-damaging greenhouse gases are either completely avoided or, if gases have been emitted, they are offset with climate protection measures (by planting trees for example).
