Behind the numbers: Why the EU looks like it’s meeting climate targets

By Bruno Dubrulle, Ruoyu Wang & Helena Robertson

We’ve all known about climate change for a while. The UN’s intergovernmental climate change body has existed for over three decades.

But only in the last couple of years has the issue been thrust into the mainstream, led by Greta Thunberg and her school climate strike that called for more meaningful action and inspired a generation of youth activists who made the world take notice.

A lot of what we read about climate change is all doom and gloom, but is there an upside? Has there been any real progress? We look into climate targets in the EU; explaining what progress has been made, what is hidden from emissions statistics, and where we might be headed in the future.

Europe has a history of progressive environmental legislation, with the earliest measures dating back to 1996, when a target was adopted to limit the global temperature rise to below 2 °C, compared to pre-industrial levels.

Currently, the EU has set itself milestones for 2020, 2030 and 2050.

By the end of 2020, the EU aims to cut 20% in greenhouse gas emissions from 1990 levels.

The EU hit this target back in 2017, when emissions were already down by 23.2%, putting them ahead of schedule.

By 2030, the EU expects to cut greenhouse gas emissions by at least 40% from 1990 levels.

In November 2019 a new European Commission, the executive branch of the EU, took office. Ursula von der Leyen, the Commission’s first-ever female president, vowed for a green, geopolitical and digital EU in her speech before Parliament.

The new European ‘Green Deal’ aims for at least a 50-55% emissions reduction by 2030...

...and committed Europe to becoming climate neutral by 2050. Climate neutrality means emissions will yield no net impact on the climate: climate-damaging greenhouse gases are either completely avoided, or they are offset with climate protection measures (by planting trees for example).

The new proposals are to be debated this summer, and if passed will become the new official EU targets.

Brexit

As negotiations on the UK’s future relationship with the EU are currently taking place, with the transition period set to end by the end of 2020, uncertainty remains as to how EU and UK climate expectations will relate to one another.

George Eustice, the UK’s environment secretary, echoed the Conservative’s campaign slogan to “Take back control”, when he said that “we will choose autonomy over regulatory alignment, we won’t be in the single market, we won’t be in the customs union”. With Prime Minister Johnson promising “full legal autonomy” for the UK, subject to supervision by the EU's climate bodies, with the reprimands and fines that go with it, seems unlikely.

In March 2018, then environment secretary Michael Gove’s talked of a ‘Green Brexit’, raising visions of the UK as a global champion in green policies. With most of the UK’s environmental regulation previously covered by EU law, the UK government will have to fill the gaps left by its departure from the continental bloc. Climate activists also fear that Brexit and the pressing discussions around trade and the UK’s global role, which might put the UK in a vulnerable position when it comes to agreeing on trade deals with countries with less stringent regulations as the UK’s, will push the climate crisis off the political agenda and stall the UK from taking action.

Much hangs on how closely the UK remains aligned to Europe. To what extent will the two collaborate on environmental and climate issues? Will the UK remain a member of the EU Emissions Trading System?

UK government officials point to domestic emission regulations which it adopted in addition to its alignment with the EU’s policies, such as the Climate Change Act of 2008 aiming to reach net-zero carbon emission by 2050 from 1990 levels.

What's included in emissions statistics?

Emissions statistics usually only include the emissions that are produced within a country. This might make sense intuitively, but calculating them this way fails to account for the emissions generated in the production of goods that are imported from elsewhere.

Industrial production has declined in the UK over time. As we've imported more goods, the UK's emissions have seemingly declined because less is produced domestically but more emissions are counted towards the countries from which we import.

Creative carbon accounting

Greenhouse gas emissions are typically measured on the basis of production, as explained above. Consumption based-reporting, on the other hand, takes into account emissions embedded in trade, including the emissions of domestic final consumption and those caused by the production of its imports.

An article published in the scientific journal PNAS in 2011, estimates that emissions from the production of traded goods and services has increased from 4.3 Gt CO2 in 1990 (20% of global emissions) to 7.8 Gt CO2 in 2008 (26%). Net emission transfers via international trade from developing to developed countries increased from 0.4 Gt CO2 in 1990 to 1.6 Gt CO2 in 2008. Whereas CO2 emissions in developed countries have stabilized, emissions in developing countries have doubled .

With countries currently reporting their emissions on production basis, it is not hard to see why this flatters statistics reported by developed regions. This sheds a different light on the emissions savings the EU has achieved so far. Although it is not possible to say whether the EU would have met its 2020 emissions target if it accounted for its import-heavy status, meeting it would have been harder.

How do we achieve climate neutrality?

The deteriorating state of the environment has attracted increasing attention around the world. Initiatives ranging from civic advocacy groups, like Extinction Rebellion, are trying to install a sense of urgency (insisting we refer to a ‘climate crisis’ instead of ‘change’), ‘woke’ or ‘enlightened’ consumers and conscious investors are pressuring companies to adopt more sustainable business models, as young people around the world are taking to the streets under Greta Thurnberg’s lead, pushing for more ambitious climate policies from governments.

The transition to climate neutrality will be gradual, but it has to start now. The debate over who will drive this transition frequently comes down to the relative importance of individuals, businesses, and governments. Each of these represent distinct opinions on how we can achieve climate neutrality in the long run. In simple terms, they respectively stand for solutions based on lifestyle (changing food consumption or choices of transport), innovation (can we invent, research or design our way out of the problem?) and regulation (with governments intervening through legislation).
